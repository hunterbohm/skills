# Scaling — Constraints, Retention, and Team

Use this guide when demand exists and the business must identify what breaks next. It is a paraphrased operating guide. Retrieve attribution from `references/evidence/scaling.md`.

## Find the constraint

Start with the current leak:

1. **Leads:** Too few qualified opportunities.
2. **Sales:** Qualified opportunities do not convert.
3. **Delivery:** Customers do not activate, succeed, stay, or repurchase.
4. **Profit:** Activity grows while contribution and cash remain weak.

Then use the doubling question as a stress test: identify what would fail if volume doubled. That exposes the next constraint; it does not replace the current diagnosis.

## Capacity

When demand exceeds supply, test these moves in order:

1. Raise price when value and demand support it.
2. Redesign delivery from one-to-one toward group, asynchronous, software-assisted, or self-service components where outcomes remain strong.
3. Standardize the work.
4. Add and train people only after the role and process are clear.

Do not add acquisition volume when onboarding, support, fulfillment, or retention is already red.

## Retention

Measure churn on a defined cohort and period. Track both customer count and revenue retained.

Improve retention by:

- moving the first meaningful win earlier;
- defining activation events correlated with staying;
- reducing onboarding delay and overwhelm;
- making progress visible;
- setting expectations before the sale;
- communicating before customers become disengaged;
- creating recurring value rather than relying on contractual friction.

Use churn thresholds as heuristics, not universal laws. Compare the business against its own cohorts, margins, contract length, and customer type.

## Unit economics

Use `LTGP:CAC`, not revenue-based LTV:CAC. More human labor and operational complexity require more margin for error. A ratio near 3:1 can be a starting heuristic for simple delivery; manual service businesses need a larger cushion.

## Team and management

Diagnose underperformance through four questions:

- **Clarity:** Is the expected result and standard explicit?
- **Capability:** Does the person know how to do it?
- **Capacity:** Do they have enough time, tools, and support?
- **Willingness:** Are incentives, feedback, and consequences aligned?

Set paired metrics so improving one cannot quietly damage another. Examples include sales with refunds, support speed with resolution quality, and delivery volume with customer success.

## Focus

Prefer more of what works, then better execution, then something new. Fragmentation across audiences, offers, and channels is itself a constraint when no engine has enough concentrated effort to become reliable.

## Completion check

A scaling recommendation is complete when it names the current constraint, the next failure under doubled volume, the capacity or retention metric to watch, and one change to make before adding more demand.
